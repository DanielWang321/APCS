Honors English 3 Final Exam Study Guide
1. MLA Formatting
Basic MLA setup
✅ Use 1-inch margins on all sides.
✅ Use 12-point Times New Roman font.
✅ Double-space the entire paper, including the heading.
✅ Do not add extra spaces between the heading, title, and paragraphs.
✅ Align paragraphs left.
✅ Indent the first line of each paragraph one tab.
MLA heading
Put this in the upper left corner of the first page:
Your First and Last Name
Teacher’s Name
Honors English 3
29 May 2026
MLA date format: day month year.
Example: 29 May 2026
MLA header
The header goes in the upper right corner of every page.
Format:
Last Name Page Number
Example:
Wang 1
Title formatting
The title of your own essay should be centered. It should not be bolded, underlined, italicized, enlarged, or put in quotation marks.

3. Incorporating Quotations
Main rule
A quotation must be part of your own sentence.
❌ Bad:
Whitman uses personification. “The last scud of day holds back for me.”
✅ Better:
Whitman uses personification when he writes, “The last scud of day holds back for me.”
Quote lead-ins
Use phrases like:
The narrator states,
Fitzgerald reveals this when he writes,
The speaker explains,
This idea appears when the author writes,
The character’s fear becomes clear when she says,
Punctuation with quotes
For normal literary quotes, the period usually goes after the parenthetical citation.
Example:
Holden admits, “I can’t stand it” (Salinger 193).
Not:
Holden admits, “I can’t stand it.” (Salinger 193)
If the quote ends with a question mark or exclamation point, keep that punctuation inside the quote, but still put a period after the citation.
Example:
Holden asks, “What Gatsby?” (Salinger 11).

4. MLA In-Text Citations
Novel citation
If the author is not named in your sentence:
Format:
(Author Page)
Example:
Holden admits, “I can’t stand it” (Salinger 193).
If the author is already named in your sentence:
Format:
(Page)
Example:
Salinger shows Holden’s frustration when he writes, “I can’t stand it” (193).
Important: Do not put a comma between the author and page number.
✅ Correct: (Cisneros 24)
❌ Wrong: (Cisneros, 24)
Poetry citation
If quoting multiple lines of poetry in a normal sentence, use a slash to show line breaks.
Example:
Whitman writes, “Failing to fetch me at first keep encouraged/Missing me one place search another” (12-13).
If the poem title is needed in the citation, put it in quotation marks.
Example:
Whitman writes, “I harbor for good or bad” (“Song of Myself” 12).
Film citation
For films, cite the film title.
Example:
Keating says, “But only in their dreams can men be truly free” (Dead Poets Society).
Online database citation
Use the author’s last name if there is one.
Example:
(Smith)
If no author is given, use a shortened version of the article title in quotation marks (first 3 words).
Example:
(“Effects of Censorship”)
For the Works Cited page, include as much publication information as the database gives you: author, article title, container title, publisher, date, database name, URL or DOI, and access date if required by your teacher.

5. Block Quotations
Use a block quotation when a quote reaches or exceeds 4 lines of your own typed paper.
Block quote rules:
✅ Keep your own quote introduction in the paragraph.
✅ Introduce the block quote with a colon.
✅ Do not use quotation marks around the block quote.
✅ Indent the quote 0.5 inch from the left margin.
✅ The punctuation stays with the quote before the citation.
✅ After the block quote, your own writing continues flush with the normal margin.
Normal quote punctuation:
“The quote ends here” (Author 45).
Block quote punctuation:
The quote ends here. (Author 45)
For poetry block quotes, keep the original line breaks. Do not use slashes.

6. Essay Structure
Introduction paragraph
A strong introduction has three parts:
Attention getter
Background information
Thesis statement
Attention getter strategies
You can begin with:
Question
Quote
Fact or statistic
Anecdote
Hypothetical situation
Definition
Literary device
Strong statement
After the attention getter, weave into the background information. Do not jump awkwardly from the hook to the thesis.
Background information
Background gives general context. It should not prove your argument yet.
For literary essays, include:
Author
Title
Basic situation or subject
Example:
In F. Scott Fitzgerald’s The Great Gatsby, the characters chase wealth, status, and love in a society shaped by illusion.
Thesis statement
A thesis is one sentence. It gives the main claim of the essay.
Single-tiered thesis
A single-tiered thesis gives only the main claim.
Example:
In The Great Gatsby, Fitzgerald shows that chasing status can destroy a person’s moral identity.
Three-tiered thesis
A three-tiered thesis gives the main claim plus three reasons or examples.
Example:
In The Great Gatsby, Fitzgerald shows that chasing status can destroy a person’s moral identity through Gatsby’s obsession with Daisy, Tom’s careless privilege, and Myrtle’s desire for wealth.

7. CER Body Paragraphs
CER stands for:
C: Claim
C2: Claim Clarification
E: Evidence
R: Reasoning
C: Claim
The claim is the topic sentence of the paragraph. It presents the subject of the paragraph.
Rules:
✅ Start with a transition word or phrase.
✅ Make it one sentence.
✅ Do not make it a question.
✅ Make sure it supports the thesis.
Example:
Furthermore, Gatsby’s obsession with Daisy shows how chasing status can replace genuine identity.
C2: Claim Clarification
C2 further explains the claim. It gives more information and leads toward the evidence.
Usually, this should be 2 to 3 sentences total.
Example:
Gatsby does not only love Daisy as a person. He also treats her as proof that he has finally entered the wealthy world he always wanted.
E: Evidence
Evidence is a quote or specific example that supports your claim.
Rules:
✅ The quote must be part of your own sentence.
✅ The quote must have a lead-in.
✅ The quote must be cited correctly.
Example:
Fitzgerald reveals Gatsby’s dream when Gatsby insists, “Can’t repeat the past? Why of course you can!” (Salinger 193).
R: Reasoning
Reasoning explains why the evidence proves the claim and thesis.
A strong reasoning section should be 3 to 4 sentences.
Use this pattern:
Says
Explain what the quote literally says.
Transition words:
In other words,
To put it differently,
Another way to say this is,
Means
Explain what the quote implies or reveals.
Transition words:
This shows,
This indicates,
This illustrates,
This proves,
Matters
Explain why it matters to the thesis or theme.
Transition words:
This matters because,
This is important because,
This demonstrates,
This connects to the theme because,

8. Conclusion Paragraph
A conclusion should have three parts:
Restate the thesis in new words.
Tie together the main points.
End with a memorable final sentence.
Do not copy the exact thesis from the introduction.
A strong final sentence should connect back to the attention getter if possible.

10. Formal Writing Rules
Do this
✅ Use third person.
✅ Use present tense when discussing literature or films.
✅ Incorporate quotes into your own sentences.
✅ Cite all quotes properly.
✅ Use MLA format.
✅ Use strong active verbs.
Do not do this
❌ Do not use first person: I, me, my, we, us, our.
❌ Do not use second person: you, your, you’re.
❌ Do not use contractions: can’t, won’t, would’ve.
❌ Do not use hanging quotes.
❌ Do not write “This essay will...”
❌ Do not overuse be verbs.
Use third person in formal writing.
Instead of:
I think Gatsby is obsessed with Daisy.
Write:
Gatsby’s actions reveal his obsession with Daisy.

11. Active Voice vs. Passive Voice
Be verbs to watch for
am
are
is
was
were
be
being
been
become
became
The active/passive notes say to use no more than 5 be verbs in essays. One quotation review says 6, but use 5 to be safer.
Active voice
The subject does the action.
Example:
The public will elect the mayor next year.
Passive voice
The subject receives the action.
Example:
A mayor will be elected by the public next year.
How to fix passive voice
Ask: Who is doing the action?
Passive:
A batch of cookies was made by my son.
Active:
My son made a batch of cookies.
Passive:
The subway fare will be raised by the city.
Active:
The city will raise the subway fare.
Even if a sentence is not technically passive, try to replace weak be verbs with stronger verbs.
Weak:
Sleep is essential for teenagers.
Stronger:
Sleep helps teenagers reduce stress.

12. Punctuation Rules
Commas
Compound sentence comma
Use a comma before a coordinating conjunction when both sides are complete sentences.
FANBOYS:
for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so
Example:
The restaurant makes delicious pasta, and the dessert menu is tempting.
Do not use a comma if the second part is not a complete sentence.
Example:
The restaurant makes delicious pasta and tempting desserts.
Series comma
Use commas to separate items in a series.
Example:
He hit the ball, dropped the bat, and ran to first base.
Introductory element comma
Use a comma after an introductory phrase or clause.
Example:
Running toward third base, he suddenly realized his mistake.
Parenthetical element comma
Use commas around extra information that can be removed without changing the main meaning.
Example:
The Founders Bridge, which spans the Connecticut River, is falling down.
Coordinate adjectives
Use a comma between adjectives if you can put “and” or “but” between them.
Example:
He is a tall, distinguished man.
Quoted elements
Use commas to set off quoted material from the sentence that introduces it.
Example:
Peter writes, “The purpose is clear.”
Contrast
Use commas for contrast.
Example:
Some say the world will end in ice, not fire.
Which vs. that
Use a comma with which when the information is extra.
Example:
The record was broken, which shocked the crowd.
Do not use a comma with that when the information is necessary.
Example:
The rooms that the designers planned were not always pleasing.
Comma splice
A comma splice happens when two complete sentences are joined with only a comma.
Wrong:
Gina’s poodle snapped at Jon, now the dog is kept on a leash.
Fix it several ways:
✅ Add FANBOYS:
Gina’s poodle snapped at Jon, so now the dog is kept on a leash.
✅ Use a semicolon:
Gina’s poodle snapped at Jon; now the dog is kept on a leash.
✅ Make two sentences:
Gina’s poodle snapped at Jon. Now the dog is kept on a leash.
✅ Use subordination:
Since Gina’s poodle snapped at Jon, it is now kept on a leash.
✅ Use a semicolon plus conjunctive adverb:
Gina’s dog snapped at Jon; consequently, it is kept on a leash.

Colons
Use a colon after a complete sentence to introduce a list.
Example:
Jenna has four cats: Angel, Cinnamon, Kit, and Whiskers.
Use a colon after a complete sentence to introduce a quote.
Example:
Reece showed his Pittsburgh vocabulary when he said these words: “I shot a gum band into the crick.”
Do not use a colon after an incomplete sentence.
Wrong:
My favorite foods are: pizza, sushi, and pasta.
Correct:
My favorite foods are pizza, sushi, and pasta.

Semicolons
Use a semicolon to join two closely related complete sentences.
Example:
Gina’s poodle snapped at Jon; now the dog is kept on a leash.
Use a semicolon with a conjunctive adverb.
Formula:
Complete sentence; however, complete sentence.
Example:
The claim seems convincing; however, the evidence is weak.
Use semicolons in a complicated series when items already contain commas.
Example:
The winners were Sarah, president; Mario, vice-president; Chelsea, secretary; and Nate, treasurer.

13. Ethos, Pathos, Logos
Ethos
Ethos builds credibility.
A writer uses ethos by:
Citing credible experts
Showing experience on the topic
Using a fair tone
Including a counterclaim and rebuttal
Showing moral understanding
Simple definition:
Ethos = credibility and trust.
Pathos
Pathos appeals to emotion.
A writer uses pathos through:
Anecdotes
Personal stories
Humor
Passion
Direct quotes
Loaded language
Emotional description
Simple definition:
Pathos = emotion.
Logos
Logos appeals to logic.
A writer uses logos through:
Facts
Data
Statistics
Logical reasoning
If-then statements
Relevant evidence
Simple definition:
Logos = logic.
A strong argument often uses all three.

14. Descriptive Essay and College App Essay Qualities
A strong descriptive or college app style essay should:
✅ Show, not just tell.
✅ Use specific details.
✅ Focus on one meaningful moment or idea.
✅ Use sensory imagery: sight, sound, smell, taste, touch.
✅ Reveal personality or growth.
✅ Avoid vague statements.
✅ Avoid sounding generic.
✅ Connect the experience to a larger meaning.
Weak:
I learned a lot from this challenge.
Stronger:
After the third failed attempt, I stopped blaming the assignment and started changing my method.

15. Works Cited Page
Use a Works Cited page when you use sources.
Basic rules:
✅ Put it on a separate page.
✅ Center the title Works Cited.
✅ Use the same MLA font, margins, spacing, and header.
✅ Double-space everything.
✅ Alphabetize entries by the author’s last name.
✅ If no author exists, alphabetize by the first important word of the title.
✅ Use hanging indent. The first line starts at the margin, and the following lines indent.
✅ Every source cited in the essay should appear on the Works Cited page.
✅ Every Works Cited entry should connect to a source used in the essay.
General article format:
Author Last Name, First Name. “Article Title.” Website or Publication Title, Publisher, Date, URL.
General book format:
Author Last Name, First Name. Book Title. Publisher, Year.

17. Rhetorical Devices
Comparison and figurative language
Simile
A comparison of two unlike things using like or as.
Example:
His smile is like the curve of a banana.
Metaphor
A comparison of two unlike things without using like or as.
Example:
The road is a ribbon around the mountain.
Analogy
A comparison used to explain or clarify something.
Example:
Life is like a box of chocolates because you never know what you will get.
Personification
Giving human qualities to something nonhuman.
Example:
The flowers danced in the wind.
Hyperbole
Extreme exaggeration for effect.
Example:
I have told you a million times.

References and wordplay
Allusion
A reference to a person, place, event, or work of literature.
Example:
His stare threw lightning bolts. This alludes to Zeus.
Types:
Literary allusion: reference to literature
Biblical allusion: reference to the Bible
Historical allusion: reference to history
Pun
A joke based on multiple meanings of a word or words that sound alike.
Example:
The orange soda dream was a Fanta sea.
Zeugma
One word, usually a verb, applies to two objects in different ways.
Example:
He stole her car and her heart.

Repetition and sound devices
Alliteration
Repetition of beginning consonant sounds.
Example:
Peter Piper picked peppers.
Consonance
Repetition of consonant sounds within or at the ends of words.
Example:
Locket and trick.
Assonance
Repetition of vowel sounds.
Example:
The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain.
Anaphora
Repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or lines.
Example:
To the house.
To the farm.
To the forest.
Epistrophe
Repetition of a word or phrase at the end of successive clauses or lines.
Example:
When I was a child, I spoke as a child, I understood as a child, I thought as a child.
Parallelism
Similar grammatical structure in nearby words, phrases, or clauses.
Example:
Give me liberty or give me death.

Contrast and structure
Paradox
A statement that seems contradictory but may reveal truth.
Example:
It was the best of times; it was the worst of times.
Irony
When reality is the opposite of what is expected.
Example:
A fire station burns down.
Antithesis
Contrasting ideas placed in balanced phrases or clauses.
Example:
It was the best of times; it was the worst of times.
Chiasmus
A sentence structure where the second clause reverses the order of ideas in the first.
Example:
Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country.

Substitution devices
Synecdoche
A part represents the whole, or the whole represents a part.
Examples:
“Wheels” means car.
“Hands” means workers.
Metonymy
A closely related word substitutes for another idea.
Example:
“The crown” means the king, queen, or royal power.

Sentence style devices
Asyndeton
Leaving out conjunctions.
Example:
I came, I saw, I conquered.
Polysyndeton
Using many conjunctions, often more than necessary.
Example:
I packed my shoes and my shirt and my jacket and my notebook.
Anecdote
A short story about a real incident or person.
Example:
A speaker tells a quick story from childhood to prove a point.
